The Techno Optimists’ Blindspot

by Prajna Prayas Last Updated: November 20, 2025

Lies.

We are being lied to. We are told that technology takes our jobs, reduces our wages, increases inequality, threatens our health, ruins the environment, degrades our society, corrupts our children, impairs our humanity, threatens our future, and is ever on the verge of ruining everything.
This is the first paragraph of much celebrated blog written by March Andressen https://a16z.com/the-techno-optimist-manifesto/

This is not a lie. This is the diagnosis of what unfettered distribution of ad-driven digital technologies have done to the collective. Automation for the sake of reducing the share of labour in the production without regard to widespread job losses and displacement of workers have been the hallmark of technological advancement for the last four decades. The consensus view that short form video and its parent global scale social media apps have degraded mental health, assaulted liberal democratic fabric built painstakingly over the last three centuries and given rise to a class of technocratic oligarchs who are not that smart as they believe themselves to be is willfully ignored by the Techno Optimists.

Blindspots are quite natural. What is worrying is such a wealthy influential caste; let alone address, but acknowledge the unintended consequences of their enabling. Some technocrats are shrewd enough to bargain a trillion dollar pay package, yet possess little intelligence to realise the debris their ego driven enterprises create in the wake of so-called progress. It's appalling to witness the collective disregard of this class to address the ills of hyper-automation-ad-driven-business model and instead focusing on amassing an obnoxious amount of wealth for themselves and their already rich shareholders.

Technology, left to its own devices, creates winners and losers. The general view amongst the intellectual and the VC class is that deploying technology is a social good and in the medium to long term beneficial to everyone; i.e. capitalists, shareholders and workers alike. While I don’t subscribe to the luddite philosophy of ‘all technology bad’, I do believe technologists have an ethical responsibility of weighing potential negative externalities their creation entails. The government, lawmakers, bureaucracy, workers, courts, civil society need to work together in building up regulatory state capacity to redirect technology into channels that generate growth and shared prosperity. The broad based sharing of prosperity is not automatic and requires countervailing forces i.e worker rights, legislation to curb monopoly, trade unions and a regulatory state. In fact for the first century of Industrial Revolution in Britain, the prosperity generated was concentrated in the hands of the wealthy capitalists, entrepreneurs and middle class while the working condition, wage, physical health worsened for most workers. The situation only improved in the second half of the 19th century when the collective bargaining power of the workers increased via trade unions, pro-worker legislations and regulatory interventions that eventually paved the way for profit sharing amongst workers.

From the paper 'The Simple Macroeconomics of AI' by Nobel prize winner Acemoglu

by Prajna Prayas Last Updated: October 24, 2025

GDP growth in industrialized nations owing to AI induced productivity boost may remain under 1% over the next 10 years.

  • Acemoglu says

    " I take Eloundou et al.’s estimates of tasks that are exposed to AI (without distinguishing automation vs. task complementarities). I then aggregate this to the occupational level and weight the importance of each occupation by its wage bill share in the US economy. This calculation implies that 19.9% of US labor tasks are exposed to AI. I then use Svanberg et al.’s estimate for computer vision tasks that, among all exposed tasks, 23% can be profitably performed by AI (for the rest, the authors estimate that the costs would exceed the benefits). I take the average labor cost savings to be 27%—the average of the estimates in Noy and Zhang (2023) and Brynjolfsson et al. (2023)—and turn this into total cost savings using industry labor shares, which imply an average total cost savings of 15.4% "

    " This calculation implies that total factor productivity (TFP) effects within the next 10 years should be no more than 0.71% in total—or approximately a 0.07% increase in TFP growth annually. If we add bigger productivity gains from Peng et al. (2023), which are less likely to be broadly applicable, or incorporate further declines in GPU costs, this number still remains around 1%."
  • He also warns about the negative social value of AI in the current state if deployed for carrying out manipulative new tasks e.g misleading ads, deepfakes even if the increase in GDP raw figures is quite substantial.
    " New tasks created with AI can more significantly boost productivity. However, some of the new AI-generated tasks are manipulative and may have negative social value, such as deepfakes, misleading digital advertisements, addictive social media or AI-powered malicious computer attacks. While it is difficult to put numbers on good and bad new tasks, based on recent research I suggest that the negative effects from new bad tasks could be sizable."
    Later he puts a speculative number to the gain in GDP and negative social value attriutable to these new (bad) tasks created by AI
    " Combining these numbers with an estimate of the fraction of activities that may generate negative social value (in practice, revenues from social media and spending on attack-defense arms races in IT security), I suggest that with more intensive use of AI, it is possible to have nontrivial increases in GDP. For example, AI may appear to increase GDP by 2%, while in reality reducing welfare by −0.72% "
